# Shipwrecks in the Roman Republic / Empire

## Motivation
Using shipwreck data from the Oxford Roman Economy Project, this project investigates patterns in recorded Roman shipwrecks to identify the most dangerous trade routes, sea areas, and geographic hotspots. The analysis is framed around a simple question: if you were a Roman sailor choosing your next assignment, how could this data help you choose 
your next assignment?

## Results

Four research questions were investigated to determine the factors a sailor might use to decide on their next voyage:

* Trade routes — Routes to and from Italy dominate the shipwreck record, but this reflects traffic volume rather than unusual danger. No single trade route stands out as categorically more deadly than others.
* Sea areas — The Western Mediterranean has the highest concentration of recorded wrecks. Again, this likely reflects the density of Roman shipping activity in that region rather than an inherent hazard specific to those waters.
* Ship size — The overwhelming majority of recorded wrecks involved vessels under 25 tonnes. Larger ships appear less frequently in the wreck record, suggesting either that they were more seaworthy or that smaller vessels were simply more numerous. Either way, bigger appears to be safer.
* Geographic hotspots — K-means clustering (k=4) identified two notably dangerous zones: a tight cluster along the southern coasts of Monaco, Sardinia, and Corsica, and a coastal concentration along Croatia and Montenegro. Ships operating in the waters around Southern Italy and the Eastern Mediterranean (Greece/Turkey region) appear comparatively safer.

### Summary of Results
 A Roman sailor would be best served by finding the largest available ship headed for the Eastern Mediterranean, and avoiding the coastal waters of the South of France and the Adriatic coast of the Balkans.
